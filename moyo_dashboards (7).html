Beta Blockers
Cardioprotective agents — reduce heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac workload
Beta-1 adrenoceptor antagonistsPost-MI · Heart failure · Hypertension · Arrhythmia
BisoprololCarvedilolMetoprololAtenololPropranolol
Mechanism of action
Beta blockers competitively block beta-1 adrenergic receptors in the heart, reducing the chronotropic (heart rate) and inotropic (contractility) response to catecholamines. This reduces myocardial oxygen demand, lowers blood pressure, and has antiarrhythmic properties. Carvedilol additionally blocks alpha-1 receptors (vasodilation) and has antioxidant properties.

Critical: Beta blockers blunt the heart rate response to exercise. Target heart rate zones calculated from formulae (220 − age) are invalid in patients on beta blockers. Borg RPE 11–14 must be used as the primary intensity guide. Do not interpret a low exercise HR as poor effort or poor fitness.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine slows your heart down and reduces the work your heart has to do. It protects your heart after a heart attack and helps it recover. Think of it as giving your heart a chance to rest and heal.
When do I take it?
Take it every day at the same time, usually in the morning with food. Never skip it without speaking to your doctor first — even if you feel well.
What might I notice?
You may feel more tired than usual, especially at first. Your hands and feet may feel colder. This is normal. Your heart will beat more slowly — do not be alarmed. If you feel dizzy when you stand up, rise slowly from sitting or lying down.
What about exercise?
This medicine means your heart rate will not rise as much during exercise as it would normally. This is expected and safe. Your exercise team will use a different scale — called the Borg scale — to guide your effort. Do not push yourself too hard just because your pulse seems low.
When should I call my doctor?
Call your doctor if your resting pulse is below 50 beats per minute, if you feel very short of breath, if your ankles swell, or if you feel faint or dizzy.
Evidence base: AHA/ACC Post-MI Guidelines 2023 · 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Heart Failure Guideline · ESC AF Guidelines 2023 · BNF 2024 · Kenya Essential Medicines List 2022
ACE Inhibitors / Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)
Neurohormonal blockade — reduce cardiac remodelling, blood pressure, and proteinuria
RAAS inhibitorsHeart failure · Post-MI · Hypertension · Diabetic nephropathy
RamiprilLisinoprilEnalaprilLosartanValsartanCandesartan
Mechanism of action
ACE inhibitors block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, reducing vasoconstriction, aldosterone secretion, and sympathetic activation. This lowers blood pressure, reduces cardiac preload and afterload, and prevents pathological cardiac remodelling post-MI. ARBs block the AT1 receptor directly — same haemodynamic effect but do not cause bradykinin accumulation (therefore no ACE inhibitor cough).

ACE inhibitors / ARBs cause vasodilation. Post-exercise hypotension risk is increased, especially in the first weeks of therapy or after dose increases. Extend cool-down to 10 minutes. Advise patients not to stand abruptly after exercise.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine relaxes your blood vessels and reduces the strain on your heart. It protects your kidneys and helps prevent your heart from enlarging after a heart attack. It is one of the most important medicines for heart recovery.
I have a cough — is it the medicine?
A dry, tickling cough that does not go away is a known side effect of ACE inhibitors (like ramipril or lisinopril). It affects about 1 in 5 patients. Tell your doctor — they may switch you to a different type of medicine (an ARB) that works the same way without causing the cough.
What might I notice?
You may feel dizzy, especially when standing up quickly. This is because the medicine lowers your blood pressure. Stand up slowly, especially in the morning. If you feel faint, sit or lie down immediately.
When should I call my doctor?
Call your doctor immediately if your lips, tongue, or throat start to swell — this is rare but can be serious. Also call if you feel unusually faint, if you stop passing urine, or if your potassium level is found to be high on a blood test.
Evidence base: 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Heart Failure Guideline · AHA/ACC Post-MI Guidelines 2023 · NICE Hypertension Guideline NG136 · Kenya Essential Medicines List 2022
Statins
Lipid-lowering agents — reduce LDL cholesterol and cardiovascular event risk
HMG-CoA reductase inhibitorsPost-MI · Atherosclerosis · Hypercholesterolaemia · Diabetes
AtorvastatinRosuvastatinSimvastatinPravastatin
Mechanism of action
Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis in the liver. This reduces hepatic cholesterol production, upregulates LDL receptors, and increases LDL clearance from the bloodstream. Beyond lipid lowering, statins have pleiotropic effects including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and plaque-stabilising properties independent of cholesterol reduction.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine lowers the amount of harmful cholesterol (LDL) in your blood. Too much LDL builds up in your arteries and can cause blockages. By reducing it, this medicine reduces your risk of having another heart attack or stroke. It also helps stabilise any existing blockages so they are less likely to rupture.
Do I need to take it forever?
Yes — for most people who have had a heart attack or have heart disease, statins are a lifelong medicine. The protection they provide only works while you are taking them. Do not stop because your cholesterol level looks normal on a test — that is because the medicine is working.
What might I notice?
Some people feel muscle aches or weakness, especially in the thighs or upper arms. This is the most common side effect. Tell your doctor or nurse if this happens — do not just stop the medicine, as there are ways to manage it, such as switching to a different statin or adjusting the dose.
When should I call my doctor urgently?
Call your doctor urgently if you have severe muscle pain all over your body AND your urine becomes dark brown — like cola. This is a rare but serious side effect that needs urgent treatment.
Evidence base: ESC/EAS Dyslipidaemia Guidelines 2023 · AHA/ACC Cholesterol Guideline 2018 (updated 2023) · AHA/AACVPR 2024 · Kenya Essential Medicines List 2022
Antiplatelet Agents
Prevent blood clots forming on coronary artery plaques and stents
Platelet aggregation inhibitorsPost-MI · Post-PCI / Stent · ACS · Stroke prevention
AspirinClopidogrelTicagrelorPrasugrel
Mechanism of action
Aspirin irreversibly inhibits COX-1, blocking thromboxane A2-mediated platelet aggregation for the lifetime of the platelet (7–10 days). Clopidogrel and ticagrelor block the P2Y12 ADP receptor on platelets, providing additional and complementary antiplatelet activity. Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) — aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor — is standard post-ACS and post-stent for 6–12 months.

Antiplatelet agents do not directly affect exercise capacity or heart rate. However, increased bleeding risk means contact sports and high-impact activities should be avoided. Patients should be advised to carry identification indicating antiplatelet use in case of accident.
Side effects to monitor
Gastric irritation / dyspepsia (aspirin) Easy bruising GI bleeding (black tarry stools) Unusual or prolonged bleeding Breathlessness (ticagrelor — reversible)
Critical safety points
Never stop DAPT without specialist advice. Stopping clopidogrel or ticagrelor prematurely after a stent risks catastrophic stent thrombosis — a life-threatening emergency. Always consult cardiology before stopping.
Aspirin + NSAIDs: Avoid ibuprofen and other NSAIDs alongside aspirin — significantly increases GI bleeding risk and may reduce aspirin's antiplatelet effect.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine stops the tiny cells in your blood called platelets from sticking together and forming dangerous clots. After a heart attack or having a stent placed, clots can form on the damaged or treated area of your artery. This medicine prevents that from happening.
Can I stop taking it if I feel fine?
No — this is extremely important. If you have had a stent placed in your artery, stopping this medicine early can cause a sudden and dangerous blockage in the stent. This can cause a very severe heart attack. Always speak to your doctor before stopping, even temporarily — including before any surgery or dental procedure.
What might I notice?
You may bruise more easily than usual. Your gums may bleed when brushing your teeth. Small cuts may take a little longer to stop bleeding. This is normal and means the medicine is working.
When should I call my doctor?
Call your doctor if your stools become black or tar-like, if you vomit blood, if you have severe stomach pain, or if you experience any unusual heavy bleeding that does not stop.
Evidence base: ESC ACS Guidelines 2023 · AHA/ACC STEMI Guidelines 2022 · ESC/EACTS Revascularisation Guidelines 2023 · Kenya Essential Medicines List 2022
Anticoagulants
Prevent stroke and clot formation in AF, valve disease, and venous thromboembolism
Coagulation pathway inhibitorsAF · Mechanical valve · DVT / PE · RHD
WarfarinRivaroxabanApixabanDabigatranEdoxaban
Mechanism of action
Warfarin inhibits vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X). Its effect is monitored via INR (target 2.0–3.0 for AF; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical valves). Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs — rivaroxaban, apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) directly inhibit factor Xa or thrombin. DOACs have predictable pharmacokinetics and do not require routine monitoring — but are not yet recommended for mechanical heart valves (warfarin remains gold standard there).

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine thins your blood to prevent dangerous clots forming. In conditions like atrial fibrillation (an irregular heartbeat) or with a heart valve, clots can form inside the heart and travel to the brain, causing a stroke. This medicine prevents that from happening.
If I am on warfarin — what about my diet?
Try to eat a consistent amount of green vegetables like spinach, kale, and broccoli. These vegetables contain vitamin K which affects how warfarin works. You do not need to avoid them — just try to eat roughly the same amount each week. Big sudden changes in your diet will affect your blood test result (INR).
What might I notice?
You will bruise more easily and cuts will take longer to stop bleeding. This is expected. Be careful with sharp objects. Wear protective footwear during exercise to avoid foot injuries.
When should I call my doctor urgently?
Seek urgent help if you have a sudden severe headache unlike any you have had before, if you vomit blood or see blood in your urine, if your stools are black and tar-like, or if you have a significant fall or bang to your head — even if you feel fine afterwards.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine helps your kidneys remove extra fluid from your body. When your heart is not pumping as strongly as it should, fluid can build up in your lungs — causing breathlessness — and in your legs and ankles — causing swelling. This medicine removes that extra fluid and helps you breathe more easily.
Why do I need to take it in the morning?
This medicine makes you pass urine frequently for a few hours after taking it. Taking it in the morning means this will happen during the day when it is easier to manage, and it will have less effect on your sleep at night.
Should I weigh myself?
Yes — weighing yourself every morning before eating or drinking, at the same time, with the same clothes, gives you and your care team valuable information. If your weight goes up by more than 1.5kg in 2 days, it likely means fluid is building up again. Contact your nurse or doctor.
What might I notice?
You will pass more urine than usual, especially in the first few hours after taking the medicine. You may feel thirsty or get leg cramps. Drink adequate water but do not overdo it. Report cramps to your clinician as they may mean your potassium level is low.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine helps control your heartbeat. Some heart conditions cause the heart to beat too fast, too slowly, or irregularly. This medicine steadies the electrical signals in your heart to keep it beating in a more regular and controlled rhythm.
I am on amiodarone — what should I know?
Amiodarone is a powerful medicine that can affect your thyroid gland, liver, lungs, and skin over time. You will need regular blood tests and check-ups every year to make sure everything is working properly. Also, your skin becomes very sensitive to sunlight — always apply high-factor sunscreen (SPF 50) and wear protective clothing when outdoors, including during exercise.
What might I notice?
Depending on your medicine, you may feel your heart beating more slowly than before. You may feel dizzy, especially in the heat or after exercise. Some medicines in this group can cause nausea, blurred vision, or light-headedness. Tell your nurse about any new symptoms.
When should I call my doctor?
Call your doctor if you notice palpitations or a very irregular pulse, if you feel faint, if you have increasing breathlessness, or if you notice your skin turning grey or you develop unexplained weight gain or weight loss (which can signal a thyroid problem).

SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins)

SGLT2 inhibitors block the sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 in the renal proximal tubule, causing urinary glucose and sodium excretion. This produces glycaemic lowering (in diabetes), osmotic diuresis (reducing cardiac preload), and natriuresis. Beyond glucose control, they have significant cardioprotective and nephroprotective effects independent of diabetes status — a major paradigm shift in heart failure treatment.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine makes your kidneys remove extra sugar and salt from your blood through your urine. It helps your heart and kidneys by reducing the strain on both organs. It has been shown to reduce hospital admissions for heart failure and slow kidney disease. Even if you do not have diabetes, it can still benefit your heart significantly.
What might I notice?
You will pass more urine than usual, especially at first. You may notice your urine smells slightly sweet — this is normal and means the medicine is working. Some people develop a fungal infection in the genital area — keeping the area clean and dry and wearing breathable cotton underwear helps prevent this.
Is there anything serious to watch for?
Rarely, this medicine can cause a serious condition called ketoacidosis — where your blood becomes too acidic. Warning signs include nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, and feeling very tired or confused, even when your blood sugar level seems normal. If you feel this way, stop the medicine and go to hospital immediately.
What about exercise?
Always eat a normal meal before exercise — do not exercise on an empty stomach while taking this medicine. Drink plenty of water during exercise. If you feel unwell with nausea or vomiting after a session, seek medical attention.

What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine relaxes and widens your blood vessels, including the arteries that supply blood to your heart. It relieves the chest pain (angina) that happens when your heart is not getting enough blood and oxygen during activity or stress.
How do I use the GTN spray for chest pain?
Sit down — do not stand up. Spray once under your tongue and close your mouth. Wait 5 minutes. If the pain does not go away, use a second spray. If the pain is still there after 2 sprays, or if it is very severe — call emergency services immediately. Do not drive yourself to hospital.
Why do I get a headache?
The same widening of blood vessels that relieves your chest pain also affects blood vessels in your head, causing a throbbing headache. This is very common and is a sign the medicine is working. It often improves after the first few weeks. Paracetamol can help. The headache is not dangerous.
Is there anything I must never combine with this medicine?
Yes — this is very important. Never take sildenafil (Viagra) or similar medicines for erectile dysfunction within 48 hours of using any nitrate medicine. The combination can cause your blood pressure to drop dangerously low, which can be life-threatening. Always tell any doctor prescribing you medication that you are on a nitrate.

MRAs competitively block aldosterone receptors in the distal nephron, reducing sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion. What to tell your patient

What does this medicine do?
This medicine blocks a hormone called aldosterone that causes your body to hold onto salt and water and damages the heart over time. By blocking it, this medicine reduces fluid in your body, helps your heart function better, and has been shown to significantly reduce the risk of death from heart failure.
Why do I need blood tests?
This medicine can raise the level of potassium in your blood — and high potassium can affect your heart rhythm. Regular blood tests check that your potassium and kidney function remain in a safe range. This is especially important in the first few weeks and after any dose change.
I am a man and I notice breast tenderness — is this the medicine?
Yes — spironolactone can sometimes cause breast tenderness or slight breast tissue development in men. This is a known side effect. Tell your doctor — they may be able to switch you to a similar medicine called eplerenone that does not cause this effect.
What should I avoid?
Avoid salt substitutes that contain potassium (such as "Lo-Salt") — these can raise your potassium to dangerous levels. Also avoid high-potassium sports drinks. Eat normally and stay well hydrated, especially when exercising in hot weather.

Why sick day rules matter: During illness, the body's physiology changes dramatically. What your patient needs to know — sick day rules

What are sick day rules?
Sick day rules are simple guidance on which of your heart medicines to stop temporarily and which ones to keep taking when you become unwell with vomiting, diarrhoea, fever, or are unable to eat or drink normally. Some medicines that are very good for your heart when you are well can cause harm when your body is dehydrated or fighting an infection.
Which medicines should I stop when I am vomiting or have diarrhoea?
If you cannot eat or drink properly due to vomiting or diarrhoea, you should temporarily STOP the following medicines until you have recovered and are eating and drinking normally again for at least 24 hours:

✓ Water tablets (furosemide, hydrochlorothiazide)
✓ ACE inhibitors or ARBs (ramipril, lisinopril, losartan, valsartan)
✓ Spironolactone or eplerenone
✓ Dapagliflozin or empagliflozin (SGLT2 tablets)
✓ Metformin (if you are diabetic)

These medicines can cause kidney problems or dangerous changes in your blood chemistry when you are dehydrated.
Which medicines must I NEVER stop on my own?
Even when you are very unwell, you must continue these medicines unless a doctor tells you otherwise:

⚠ Your beta blocker (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol, atenolol) — stopping suddenly can be dangerous
⚠ Your blood thinners (aspirin, clopidogrel, ticagrelor, warfarin, rivaroxaban) — stopping can cause a clot or stroke
⚠ Your statin (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin)

If you cannot swallow tablets, call your doctor or nurse immediately.
When should I go to hospital immediately?
Go to hospital straight away if you have chest pain, severe difficulty breathing, feel faint, cannot keep any fluids or medicines down for more than 24 hours, or if you are vomiting and feel confused or extremely weak while taking your SGLT2 tablet.
What about before an operation?
Before any planned operation or procedure, always tell the surgeon and anaesthetist all the heart medicines you are taking. Some medicines need to be stopped a few days before surgery and others should be continued. Your surgical team will give you specific instructions — follow them carefully and contact your heart nurse if you are unsure.
When can I restart my medicines?
Restart the medicines you stopped once you have been eating and drinking normally for 24–48 hours, you are no longer vomiting or having diarrhoea, and you feel back to your usual self. If you are unsure, call your nurse or doctor before restarting.

Cooking oil (sunflower, palm oil) Use sparingly — choose wisely
Use small amounts. Sunflower, rapeseed, and olive oil are preferable to palm oil (saturated fat). Avoid deep-frying. Use 1–2 tablespoons per meal maximum. Never reuse cooking oil.

Very high in sugar. Raise blood sugar rapidly, contribute to weight gain, and worsen triglycerides. Replace with water, plain uji (unsweetened), black tea without sugar, or fresh lemon water. Dramatically important for diabetic patients.
Salt reduction — practical tips for Kenyan cooking
The biggest change you can make is reducing salt. Here is how to do it in a Kenyan kitchen:

Replace stock cubes (Royco) with onions, garlic, ginger, tomatoes, and coriander for flavour
Use lemon or lime juice to add flavour without salt
Cook beans and lentils from scratch rather than using salted tinned versions
Reduce salt in cooking gradually over 2–4 weeks — your taste buds adapt within a few weeks
Read labels on packaged foods — anything with more than 0.6g sodium per 100g is high in salt
Avoid or greatly reduce: Royco cubes, soy sauce, salted crisps, salted nuts, and pickled vegetables
Special dietary guidance by condition
Heart failure patients — fluid and salt: If your clinician has advised fluid restriction, follow their specific guidance on daily fluid limits. In general: reduce salt aggressively (fluid follows salt in the body), weigh daily, and avoid high-sodium foods. Limit tea, uji, and soup portions if fluid-restricted.
Diabetes + cardiac disease: Avoid all sugary drinks, white ugali in large portions, white bread, and high-glycaemic foods. Increase beans, lentils, vegetables, and whole grains. Check blood sugar regularly around meals. Eat at regular times and never skip meals.
High blood pressure: Reduce salt aggressively — this is the single most effective dietary change. Increase potassium-rich foods: sukuma wiki, bananas, sweet potatoes, beans, and avocado. Potassium helps offset the blood-pressure-raising effect of sodium.
High cholesterol: Reduce saturated fats from meat, full-fat dairy, and palm oil. Increase soluble fibre from oats, beans, lentils, and vegetables — soluble fibre directly lowers LDL cholesterol. Increase fish consumption to 2–3 times per week.
Anticoagulant patients (warfarin): Do not avoid vitamin K vegetables (sukuma wiki, kale, spinach) — but eat a consistent amount each week. Sudden large changes in green vegetable intake affect your INR. Consistency matters more than restriction.

Why does lifestyle matter as much as medicine?
Your medicines are essential — but they work best alongside healthy lifestyle habits. Research consistently shows that patients who combine medication adherence with lifestyle change have significantly better long-term outcomes than those who rely on medication alone. The habits you build during your cardiac rehabilitation programme will protect your heart for the rest of your life.
Physical activity
How much activity should I aim for?
The World Health Organisation recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week for adults with cardiovascular disease — that is 30 minutes on 5 days per week. You do not need a gym. Walking is the most accessible, most evidence-based, and most sustainable form of exercise for cardiac patients in Kenya. Build up gradually from where you are now — your Moyo Smart programme guides you week by week. On days when full sessions are not possible, even 10 minutes of gentle walking is better than nothing and contributes to your recovery.
Exercise in Kenya — practical tips:
Walk early in the morning (before 9am) or in the evening (after 5pm) to avoid peak heat and humidity
Flat, shaded routes are ideal — avoid steep hills in the early weeks
Wear comfortable, supportive shoes — bare feet on uneven ground increase injury risk
Bring water — dehydration in the Kenyan climate is a real risk and worsens cardiac symptoms
Tell a family member where you are going and when to expect you back
Carry your Moyo Smart emergency contact card during every exercise session
Sleep
How does sleep affect my heart?
Poor sleep is a significant but underrecognised cardiovascular risk factor. Less than 6 hours of sleep per night is associated with increased blood pressure, elevated stress hormones, weight gain, and higher risk of heart attack and stroke. Aim for 7–9 hours of good quality sleep each night. If you snore loudly, wake up unrefreshed, or feel excessively sleepy during the day, discuss this with your clinician — you may have obstructive sleep apnoea, which is common in cardiac patients and very treatable.
For heart failure patients: If you wake up gasping for breath or cannot lie flat without feeling breathless, this is a medical symptom called paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea or orthopnoea. It indicates worsening fluid in the lungs. Contact your clinician immediately — do not wait for your next appointment.
Alcohol
Is it safe to drink alcohol with my heart condition?
Alcohol is a direct cardiac toxin at higher doses. It raises blood pressure, can trigger arrhythmias (particularly atrial fibrillation), worsens heart failure, interacts with cardiac medications including warfarin, and contributes to weight gain and diabetes. If you currently drink alcohol:

Heart failure, arrhythmia, or cardiomyopathy: Strongly advised to stop completely. Alcohol directly worsens these conditions.
Post-MI, post-stent, or post-surgery: Maximum 1 unit per day for women, 2 units per day for men — with alcohol-free days. Less is always better.
On warfarin: Heavy alcohol use raises INR unpredictably. Moderation is essential.

Traditional fermented drinks (busaa, changaa) and illicitly brewed spirits carry additional risks due to variable alcohol content and potential contaminants — these should be avoided entirely.
Work and daily activities
When can I return to work?
This depends on your condition and type of work. General guidance:

Desk-based or light work: Usually 2–4 weeks after a heart attack or stent procedure. 6–8 weeks after surgery.
Moderate physical work: Usually 4–8 weeks depending on recovery and cardiac function.
Heavy manual labour: 8–12 weeks minimum, and only with explicit cardiology clearance. Some roles may require permanent modification.
Driving: 4 weeks after a heart attack or stent (private vehicle). 6 weeks after surgery. 4 weeks after ICD implantation (no episode). Commercial drivers — discuss with your NTSA-registered doctor and cardiologist.

Always discuss your specific occupation with your clinician before returning to work.
Sexual activity
When can I resume sexual activity?
Sexual activity is generally safe from 4–6 weeks after a heart attack or stent procedure, and 6–8 weeks after cardiac surgery, provided you can climb two flights of stairs or walk briskly for 10 minutes without chest pain or excessive breathlessness. The energy demand of sexual activity is equivalent to moderate exercise. If chest pain or significant breathlessness occurs during sexual activity, stop and rest — and discuss with your clinician. If you take nitrates (GTN spray), never take PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/Viagra, tadalafil/Cialis) — the combination can cause life-threatening low blood pressure.
Stress management
How does stress affect my heart?
Chronic stress raises cortisol and adrenaline levels, increasing heart rate and blood pressure, promoting inflammation, and contributing to unhealthy behaviours like poor eating, alcohol use, and smoking. Acute emotional stress can trigger cardiac events — including heart attacks — in vulnerable patients. Managing stress is not a luxury, it is a clinical priority. Practical strategies available in the Kenyan context include:

Regular moderate exercise (already part of your programme)
Prayer and spiritual practice — important for many Kenyan patients and associated with better cardiac outcomes
Social connection — maintaining relationships with family and friends is protective
Community group participation
Breathing exercises and relaxation techniques
Speaking with your Moyo Smart psychosocial support team (see Psychosocial Support tab)
Weight management
Target BMI: 18.5–24.9 kg/m²
Being overweight or obese increases blood pressure, worsens diabetes, increases strain on the heart, and raises cholesterol. Even a 5–10% reduction in body weight produces significant cardiovascular benefit. Focus on sustainable, gradual weight loss through reduced portion sizes, healthier food choices, and regular exercise — not crash diets, which are dangerous in cardiac patients. Discuss weight targets with your clinician and rehabilitation team.

What is a cardiovascular risk factor?
A risk factor is a condition or habit that increases your chance of having a heart attack or stroke. Some risk factors — like age and family history — cannot be changed. But the most powerful risk factors are ones you can directly influence: blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar, weight, smoking, and physical inactivity. Controlling these factors after a cardiac event is as important as taking your medicines. Each risk factor you control reduces your overall risk significantly.

Blood pressure — the silent killer
Why is controlling blood pressure so important?
High blood pressure (hypertension) is the single most modifiable cardiovascular risk factor in Kenya. It often has no symptoms — which is why it is called the silent killer. Every 10 mmHg reduction in systolic blood pressure reduces the risk of stroke by 35% and heart attack by 20%. You may be on one or more blood pressure medicines as part of your cardiac treatment. These must be taken every day without fail, even when you feel well — especially when you feel well, because that may mean they are working.
How to measure your blood pressure correctly:
Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measuring
Sit with your back supported and feet flat on the floor
Place the cuff on your bare upper arm at heart level
Do not talk during the measurement
Take two readings 1–2 minutes apart and record the average
Measure at the same time each day — morning before medicines is ideal
Bring your readings to every clinic visit
Cholesterol — understanding your numbers
What is LDL and why is it dangerous?
LDL (low-density lipoprotein) is sometimes called "bad cholesterol" because it deposits into artery walls and forms plaques — the same plaques that caused your cardiac event. Reducing LDL cholesterol is one of the most powerful interventions available in cardiac secondary prevention. Your statin medication is the primary treatment — but diet also plays a significant role. Every 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL reduces the risk of a major cardiac event by approximately 22%. This is why your statin must be taken every day for life.

Blood sugar and diabetes
I have diabetes — how does this affect my heart recovery?
Diabetes is one of the most powerful accelerators of cardiovascular disease. High blood sugar damages blood vessels, promotes inflammation, and accelerates atherosclerosis. After a cardiac event, diabetes management becomes even more critical. Good blood sugar control reduces the risk of further cardiac events, protects your kidneys, preserves your vision, and prevents nerve damage. Take your diabetes medications every day. Weight and waist circumference
How does excess weight affect my heart?
Excess body fat — particularly fat stored around the abdomen — directly raises blood pressure, worsens cholesterol, promotes insulin resistance (leading to diabetes), and increases inflammation. Abdominal obesity is highly prevalent in Kenya and is a major driver of the cardiovascular disease epidemic. You do not need to reach an ideal weight to gain benefit — losing just 5–10% of your current body weight produces significant improvements in blood pressure, blood sugar, and cholesterol within weeks. Focus on gradual, sustainable loss through portion control and regular exercise.
Family history and non-modifiable risks
If close family members (parents, siblings) have had heart attacks before age 55 (men) or 65 (women), you have a higher genetic risk. This cannot be changed — but it means controlling your modifiable risk factors is even more important. It also means your first-degree relatives should be screened for high blood pressure, cholesterol, and diabetes. Encourage them to have a health check.
Stopping Smoking — The Single Most Important Thing You Can Do
Evidence-based support for smoking cessation after a cardiac event

Why is stopping smoking the single most important change I can make?
Smoking is the most powerful reversible risk factor for cardiovascular disease. It damages blood vessel walls, promotes clot formation, raises blood pressure, reduces HDL (good) cholesterol, causes coronary artery spasm, and dramatically accelerates atherosclerosis. If you have had a heart attack, continuing to smoke doubles your risk of a second heart attack within 5 years. Stopping smoking, even after a cardiac event, produces dramatic and rapid cardiovascular benefits — within weeks, not years.
The evidence is unambiguous:

Stopping smoking after a heart attack reduces risk of reinfarction and cardiac death by up to 50% within 2 years — more than any single medication
Within 20 minutes of stopping: heart rate and blood pressure drop
Within 12 hours: carbon monoxide levels in blood return to normal
Within 2–12 weeks: circulation improves, exercise becomes easier
Within 1 year: excess risk of coronary artery disease is halved
Within 15 years: cardiovascular risk approaches that of a non-smoker
Is it too late to stop now that I have already had a heart attack?
Absolutely not — it is never too late, and the sooner you stop the greater the benefit. The body has a remarkable ability to recover after smoking cessation, even in patients with established heart disease. Many patients find that a cardiac event is the most powerful motivator to stop — use this moment. Your Moyo Smart team will support you throughout the process.
What makes stopping smoking difficult
Why is it so hard to stop?
Nicotine is one of the most addictive substances known. It acts on the brain's reward system in a similar way to addictive drugs. Physical withdrawal symptoms — irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, increased appetite, and intense cravings — peak within 24–72 hours of stopping and typically resolve within 2–4 weeks. Psychological dependence (the habit and ritual of smoking) often lasts longer. Both components need to be addressed for successful cessation. You are not weak if you find it hard — you are dealing with a genuine physical addiction.
Your step-by-step quit plan
  • Set a quit date — within 2 weeks
    Choose a specific date. Write it down. Tell your family and friends. Remove all cigarettes, tobacco, and ashtrays from your home the day before your quit date.
  • Identify your triggers
    Common triggers in Kenya: after meals, with tea, during stress, at social gatherings, when drinking alcohol. Write yours down. Plan specifically what you will do instead at each trigger moment.
  • Consider nicotine replacement therapy (NRT)
    NRT doubles your chances of success. Options available in Kenya include nicotine patches (applied to skin), nicotine gum, and nicotine lozenges. Available at major pharmacies. Discuss with your clinician or pharmacist.
  • Tell your Moyo Smart team
    Your rehabilitation team will provide structured smoking cessation support as part of your programme. Brief counselling at each session significantly improves quit rates.
  • Manage the first 72 hours
    The first 3 days are the hardest. Cravings last 3–5 minutes. When a craving hits: drink water, take 10 deep breaths, walk away from the situation, call a supportive friend or family member. Cravings always pass — you just need to outlast each one.
  • Handle social pressure
    Smoking is social in many Kenyan settings. Prepare a firm but polite response: "I have had a heart problem and I cannot smoke anymore." Most people will respect this. Avoid smoking environments in the first few weeks if possible.
  • If you relapse — do not give up
    Most people take several attempts before successfully stopping for good. A relapse is not failure — it is information. Identify what triggered it and plan differently for next time. Contact your Moyo Smart team immediately for renewed support.

Your Mental Health Matters Too

Is it normal to feel anxious or low after a cardiac event?
Completely and absolutely normal. A cardiac event — whether a heart attack, surgery, or new diagnosis — is one of the most frightening and life-altering experiences a person can have. It is a confrontation with mortality, a disruption to daily life, and often a source of significant fear, uncertainty, and grief. Up to 30% of people develop clinical depression after a heart attack, and up to 35% experience clinically significant anxiety. These are not signs of weakness — they are predictable physiological and psychological responses to a major health crisis. And they are treatable.
How does psychological distress affect my physical recovery?
Depression and anxiety after a cardiac event are not just matters of emotional comfort — they directly worsen physical outcomes. Patients with untreated depression after a heart attack have two to three times higher risk of a second event compared to those without depression. Psychological distress increases inflammation, raises stress hormones, disrupts sleep, reduces medication adherence, and is the single strongest predictor of dropping out of cardiac rehabilitation. Addressing your mental health is not optional — it is part of your cardiac treatment.

  • 💭Fear: Fear of another event, fear of exercise, fear of dying. Very common. Your rehabilitation team will help you gradually rebuild confidence through safe, structured activity.

  • 💭Grief: Grief for your previous health, your physical abilities, and your sense of invulnerability. A genuine loss that deserves acknowledgement and support.
  • 💭Guilt: Guilt about lifestyle choices, about being a burden on family, or about financial impact of illness. Common — and rarely proportionate to reality.
  • 💭Low mood and withdrawal: Loss of interest in activities, withdrawing from family and friends, feeling hopeless about recovery. Important to report to your team — these are signs of depression.

Understanding Your Heart Attack
A guide for patients and families following a myocardial infarction (MI)
Myocardial Infarction STEMI / NSTEMI Cardiac Rehabilitation
What happened to my heart?
Your heart is a muscle that needs a constant supply of blood and oxygen to work. Blood is delivered to the heart through vessels called coronary arteries. Over time, fatty deposits called plaque can build up inside these arteries — a process called atherosclerosis. A heart attack happens when one of these plaques suddenly bursts open. Your body tries to repair it by forming a blood clot, but this clot blocks the artery completely. Without blood flow, the part of the heart muscle supplied by that artery begins to die. The faster blood flow is restored, the less damage occurs. This is why rapid treatment is so critical.
What was done to treat it?
Depending on how quickly you arrived at hospital and what type of heart attack you had, treatment may have included one or more of the following:

Emergency stent (primary PCI): A wire was passed into the blocked artery and a small mesh tube (stent) was inserted to open it and hold it open. This is the fastest way to restore blood flow.

Clot-busting medicine (thrombolysis): A powerful medicine was given to dissolve the clot. This is used when stenting is not immediately available.

Medications: You were given aspirin, other blood thinners, and medicines to stabilise your heart immediately. You will continue many of these medicines long-term — they are essential for your recovery and prevention of another heart attack.
How much damage was done?
Every heart attack is different. Some cause small areas of damage; others affect a larger area of the heart muscle. Your doctors will have assessed this using an echocardiogram (heart scan) and blood tests. Even if there has been some permanent damage to part of your heart muscle, your heart is remarkably adaptable. With the right medicines, rehabilitation, and lifestyle changes, many people make a full functional recovery and live active, fulfilling lives.
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation?
Cardiac rehabilitation is not just exercise — it is a structured programme that helps your heart heal, reduces your risk of having another heart attack, and helps you return to normal life safely. The evidence is clear and compelling:
Why cardiac rehabilitation saves lives after a heart attack
42%
Reduction in risk of readmission or death within 180 days of a heart attack with cardiac rehabilitation
25%
Reduction in long-term risk of another heart attack with regular structured exercise
1 in 3
Heart attack survivors develop depression — cardiac rehabilitation addresses both the physical and emotional recovery
When is the best time to start cardiac rehabilitation?
The sooner the better — within 7–14 days of discharge from hospital for most stable patients. Early rehabilitation is safe and significantly more effective than starting late. Research shows that patients who begin rehabilitation within 2 weeks of a heart attack have better outcomes than those who wait months. Do not wait until you feel completely well — the rehabilitation programme is designed to safely guide you back to activity from where you are now.
Before you start — what to watch for
  • ⛔Stop all activity and call emergency services immediately if: You have chest pain or tightness · Severe breathlessness at rest · Sudden loss of consciousness or collapse · Palpitations that feel very fast, irregular, or are accompanied by dizziness

Understanding Heart Failure
A guide for patients and families living with heart failure
Heart Failure HFrEF / HFpEF Cardiac Rehabilitation
What is heart failure?
Heart failure does not mean your heart has stopped working — it means your heart is not pumping as strongly as it should be. Think of the heart as a pump. In heart failure, that pump has become weakened or stiff, meaning it cannot push enough blood around your body with each beat. This leads to your body not getting enough oxygen and nutrients, and fluid starting to build up in the lungs and legs. Heart failure is a serious but manageable condition. With the right treatment and lifestyle changes, many people live well for many years.
What caused my heart failure?
Heart failure is usually caused by damage to or extra strain on the heart over time. Common causes include:

Coronary artery disease / previous heart attack — the most common cause. Damaged heart muscle from a previous heart attack pumps less efficiently.

High blood pressure (hypertension) — forces the heart to work harder over many years, eventually weakening it.

Heart valve disease — leaking or narrowed valves increase the workload on the heart.

Cardiomyopathy — a disease of the heart muscle itself, sometimes inherited.

Diabetes — damages blood vessels and directly affects heart muscle function over time.

In some cases, more than one cause is present. Your clinical team can explain which factors contributed to your heart failure.
What does my ejection fraction number mean?
Your ejection fraction (EF) is a measurement of how much blood your heart pumps out with each beat, expressed as a percentage. A normal EF is 55–70%. An EF below 40% means your heart is pumping significantly less blood than normal (this is called HFrEF — heart failure with reduced ejection fraction). An EF above 50% with heart failure symptoms is called HFpEF — where the heart muscle is stiff rather than weak. Your EF is one of many measurements your team uses to guide your treatment. Even with a low EF, exercise and medication can significantly improve how you feel and function.
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation?
Exercise is one of the most powerful treatments available for heart failure — more powerful than many medications for quality of life. Exercise training strengthens the heart, improves the efficiency of your circulation, reduces breathlessness, improves your energy levels, and reduces the risk of hospitalisation. It was previously believed that heart failure patients should rest and avoid exercise. This has been completely overturned by modern evidence. Structured, supervised exercise is now a Class IA recommendation — the highest level of medical evidence — for heart failure patients who are stable.

When is the best time to start?

Do not start during an acute flare. Once stable, starting within 2–4 weeks of discharge is ideal. Starting early, even with very short sessions of 5–10 minutes, is better than waiting until you feel completely well.

Understanding Your Heart Bypass Surgery
A guide for patients following coronary artery bypass graft surgery (CABG)
CABG Surgery Coronary Artery Disease Cardiac Rehabilitation
What is coronary artery bypass surgery?
Your coronary arteries supply blood to your heart muscle. Over time, fatty deposits can narrow or block these arteries. Bypass surgery creates new routes — bypasses — around the blocked sections, restoring blood flow to the heart. Your surgeon takes a blood vessel from another part of your body — usually from your leg (saphenous vein), chest wall (internal mammary artery), or arm — and uses it to create a new pathway for blood to flow around the blockage. The number of bypasses performed reflects how many arteries were significantly blocked — you may have had 1, 2, 3, or more bypasses.
Why did I need this operation?
Bypass surgery is recommended when coronary arteries are too severely blocked for a stent to be safely placed, when multiple arteries are significantly narrowed, or when the main artery supplying the heart (left main coronary artery) is critically narrowed. Surgery provides longer-lasting relief from angina and in many patients significantly reduces the risk of a future heart attack compared to medication alone.
What is a sternal wound and why does it need special care?
To access your heart, the surgeon divided your breastbone (sternum) down the middle. This is called a median sternotomy. The sternum was then wired back together after the operation. It takes approximately 6–8 weeks for the sternum to fully heal and become solid. During this time, the breastbone is vulnerable — putting too much stress on it can cause it to move, which is painful, can delay healing, and in rare cases requires surgical revision. This is why sternal precautions are absolutely non-negotiable in the early weeks.
Sternal precautions — strictly follow for 6–8 weeks after surgery:

Do not lift anything heavier than a cup of tea or a light book (no more than 1–2kg)
Do not push or pull with your arms — no pushing yourself up from a chair using your arms
Do not reach above shoulder height
Do not carry shopping, bags, or children
Do not drive for at least 6 weeks
Do not sleep on your side in the first weeks — sleep on your back with pillows for support

Report immediately if: Your wound becomes red, swollen, hot, or begins to discharge fluid · You feel or hear clicking in your chest · You have increasing pain at the wound site
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation after bypass surgery?
Bypass surgery treats the blockages but does not cure the underlying disease — atherosclerosis continues if lifestyle and risk factors are not addressed. Cardiac rehabilitation helps you recover safely from major surgery, strengthens your heart and body, and most importantly reduces the risk of future blockages forming. Without rehabilitation, many patients decondition significantly in the first months after surgery and are at higher risk of readmission.
When is the best time to start rehabilitation?
You can begin gentle walking as early as the first week after discharge — short, flat walks are safe and beneficial from the start. Your full cardiac rehabilitation programme with the Moyo Smart team should begin at 6–8 weeks, once your sternal wound is healed and your surgeon has confirmed you are cleared for structured exercise. Do not wait until you feel completely back to normal — by 6–8 weeks you will be ready to begin and the rehabilitation will accelerate your recovery significantly.

Understanding Your Heart Valve Surgery
A guide for patients following valve repair or replacement
Valve Repair Valve Replacement Cardiac Rehabilitation
What do heart valves do?
Your heart has four valves — the mitral, aortic, tricuspid, and pulmonary valves. They act like one-way doors, opening and closing with each heartbeat to ensure blood flows in the right direction. When a valve becomes narrowed (stenosis) or leaky (regurgitation), the heart has to work much harder and blood does not flow as efficiently. Over time this strains the heart and causes symptoms like breathlessness, fatigue, and swelling.
Was my valve repaired or replaced?
Valve repair — your surgeon reshaped, trimmed, or reinforced your own valve to help it work properly. This is always preferred when possible because your own tissue works best.

Mechanical valve replacement — a durable artificial valve made of metal and carbon was implanted. Mechanical valves last a lifetime but require you to take a blood thinner (warfarin) for life. You may hear a soft clicking sound from the valve — this is normal.

Bioprosthetic valve replacement — a valve made from animal tissue (usually pig or cow) was implanted. Bioprosthetic valves do not usually require lifelong blood thinners but may need replacing after 10–15 years.
If I have a mechanical valve — what must I know?
Warfarin (your blood thinner) is non-negotiable with a mechanical valve. Without it, clots can form on the valve and travel to the brain, causing a devastating stroke. You will need regular INR blood tests to ensure your blood is at the right thickness. Your target INR is usually 2.5–3.5 depending on which valve was replaced. Never stop warfarin without specialist advice — even for a few days — without first speaking to your cardiologist. Always inform any doctor, dentist, or surgeon that you have a mechanical heart valve.
Sternal precautions apply after valve surgery (same as bypass surgery):
No lifting more than 1–2kg for 6–8 weeks · No pushing or pulling with arms · No reaching above shoulder height · No driving for 6 weeks · Report wound redness, discharge, or clicking sounds from the chest immediately.
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation?
Heart valve disease and its surgical treatment often leave the heart deconditioned after months or years of reduced function. Cardiac rehabilitation helps restore your heart's strength and efficiency, rebuilds your physical fitness safely after major surgery, and helps you manage the long-term aspects of living with a replaced or repaired valve. It also provides important education about anticoagulation management, endocarditis prevention, and recognising signs of valve problems.
When is the best time to start?
Gentle walking from week 1–2 post-discharge. Structured cardiac rehabilitation at 6–8 weeks once surgical clearance is confirmed. Do not wait longer than 10–12 weeks to begin — the longer the delay, the more deconditioned you become and the harder recovery feels.
Endocarditis prevention — vital for all valve patients
Endocarditis is an infection of the heart valves. It is a serious, life-threatening condition. Valve patients are at higher risk. Good oral hygiene is your single most important protection: Brush teeth twice daily · Floss regularly · See a dentist every 6 months · Tell every dentist and doctor that you have a heart valve condition. You may need antibiotic cover before dental procedures — ask your cardiologist. Report any unexplained fever, night sweats, or feeling persistently unwell to your doctor immediately.
Before starting rehabilitation — warning signs
  • ⛔Seek emergency care immediately for: New breathlessness at rest · Chest pain · Fainting · Rapidly worsening breathlessness or fluid build-up · Any unusual sound from your mechanical valve changing
  • ⚠Contact your clinician if: INR is out of the target range · New ankle swelling · Unexplained fever · Any signs of wound infection
Understanding Your Stent Procedure
A guide for patients following percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and coronary stent placement
PCI / Stent Coronary Artery Disease Cardiac Rehabilitation
What is a coronary stent?
A stent is a tiny, expandable mesh tube — about the size of a short piece of pen spring — made of metal. During your procedure, a thin flexible tube (catheter) was guided through a blood vessel in your wrist (radial approach) or groin (femoral approach) to reach the blocked coronary artery. A balloon on the end of the catheter was inflated to open the blockage, and the stent was deployed to hold the artery open permanently. Most modern stents are drug-eluting stents (DES) — they are coated with a medication that slowly releases into the artery wall to prevent scar tissue from re-blocking it.
Why is my blood thinning medication so important?
When a stent is first placed, the metal surface can attract blood cells that form clots — a process called stent thrombosis. This is a rare but catastrophic event that usually causes a very severe heart attack. To prevent it, you must take dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) — aspirin plus a second blood thinner (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel) — for a minimum of 6–12 months. Never stop either medicine without first speaking to your cardiologist — even if you need an operation or procedure. The risk of stopping is far greater than continuing in almost all situations.
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation after a stent?
A stent opens the blocked artery — but it does not treat the underlying atherosclerosis or the risk factors that caused the blockage. Without rehabilitation and lifestyle change, new blockages will form in the same or other arteries. Cardiac rehabilitation after a stent reduces the risk of another heart attack, improves fitness, and provides structured support to address the factors — smoking, diet, blood pressure, cholesterol, diabetes, stress — that caused the problem in the first place.

If your stent was for stable angina, you can start even sooner. There are no sternal restrictions after a stent — your chest was not opened. Your main restriction is at the access site (wrist or groin) which heals within a few days.

Understanding Your ICD or Pacemaker
A guide for patients following implantation of an ICD or permanent pacemaker
ICD Pacemaker CRT Device Cardiac Rehabilitation
What is a pacemaker?
A pacemaker is a small battery-powered device implanted just below your collarbone. It monitors your heart rhythm continuously. If your heart beats too slowly, the pacemaker sends small electrical impulses to stimulate the heart to beat at the correct rate. Modern pacemakers are highly reliable, very small, and last 7–10 years before the battery needs replacing. You will generally not feel the pacemaker delivering its impulses — most patients feel normal and can return to full activity.
What is an ICD and how is it different from a pacemaker?
An ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator) also monitors your heart rhythm continuously. However, it is designed to detect and treat dangerously fast heart rhythms (ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation) that can cause sudden cardiac arrest. If the ICD detects a dangerous rhythm, it delivers a shock to reset the heart to a normal rhythm. This can feel like a sudden strong thump to the chest — it is startling but life-saving. Many ICDs also have pacemaker functions built in. You may have received an ICD because of previous cardiac arrest, severely reduced heart function, or dangerous arrhythmias.
What is a CRT device?
CRT (cardiac resynchronisation therapy) is a special type of pacemaker that sends impulses to both sides of the heart simultaneously, improving the coordination of the heart's pumping action. It is used in patients with heart failure whose heart walls are not contracting in sync. CRT can significantly improve symptoms, exercise capacity, and quality of life in appropriate patients. Your device may be a CRT-P (pacing only) or CRT-D (pacing plus defibrillation).
Device implant precautions — strictly follow for 6–8 weeks after implantation:

Do NOT raise your arm on the device side above shoulder height — this is the single most critical restriction. Overhead movement can displace the device leads before they are secured in place.

Do not lift anything heavier than 2–3kg on the device side arm
Do not push or pull with the device side arm
Do not sleep on your side on the device side for 4–6 weeks
Do not drive for 1 week after pacemaker or 4 weeks after ICD implantation (longer if shock was delivered)
Do not immerse the wound in water until fully healed (no swimming for 4–6 weeks)

Report immediately if: Wound becomes red, swollen, or discharging · You receive a shock from your ICD · You feel dizzy, faint, or notice a very irregular pulse · Hiccups that will not stop (can indicate lead displacement)
If my ICD fires — what should I do?
If you receive a single shock and feel well immediately after — sit down, rest, and call your device clinic or clinician to report it. It may have been appropriate (treating a dangerous arrhythmia) or inappropriate (triggered by exercise or electromagnetic interference). If you receive multiple shocks, feel unwell, or remain symptomatic — call emergency services immediately. Always carry your device identification card and inform emergency staff of your ICD.
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation?
Exercise is safe and highly beneficial for device patients. Many patients feel anxious about exercising after receiving a device — particularly ICD patients who fear triggering a shock. This fear is understandable but should not prevent you from exercising. Your cardiac rehabilitation team will work within safe heart rate limits below your ICD detection zone. Exercise improves heart function, reduces arrhythmia burden, improves quality of life, and reduces the risk of inappropriate ICD shocks by improving fitness.
When is the best time to start?
Gentle walking can begin from week 1–2. Structured cardiac rehabilitation should start at 4–8 weeks once the device wound has healed and your cardiologist has confirmed the leads are secure. Before starting exercise, your cardiac rehabilitation team must be informed of your device type and the ICD detection threshold — your exercise heart rate must stay at least 20–30 bpm below this threshold at all times.

Understanding Rheumatic Heart Disease
A guide for patients and families living with rheumatic heart disease (RHD) in Kenya
Rheumatic Heart Disease Mitral Valve Disease Cardiac Rehabilitation
What is rheumatic heart disease?
Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) develops as a long-term consequence of rheumatic fever — an inflammatory condition that follows untreated or inadequately treated throat infections caused by a bacterium called Group A Streptococcus. When rheumatic fever affects the heart, it causes inflammation of the heart valves. Over time — sometimes years or decades after the original infection — this inflammation causes the valves to become thickened, scarred, and either too narrow (stenosis) or leaky (regurgitation). RHD is far more common in Kenya and Sub-Saharan Africa than in high-income countries, and it frequently affects younger people — including children, teenagers, and working-age adults.
Which valve is affected and what does this mean?
Mitral stenosis — the mitral valve (between the left upper and lower chambers) is too narrow. Blood cannot pass through efficiently, causing pressure to build in the lungs. This causes breathlessness, especially with exertion, and risk of atrial fibrillation (irregular heartbeat) and stroke.

Mitral regurgitation — the mitral valve leaks, allowing blood to flow backwards with each heartbeat. This forces the heart to work harder and can eventually weaken it.

Aortic valve disease — the aortic valve (the main exit valve of the heart) may also be affected, causing similar problems of narrowing or leakage.

Many patients with RHD have more than one valve affected and more than one type of problem.
Why is penicillin (secondary prophylaxis) so important?
Every episode of streptococcal throat infection can cause additional damage to your already-affected valve. Monthly penicillin injections or daily oral penicillin prevent re-infection and further valve damage. This secondary prophylaxis is the most important medicine you take for your heart — more important than any other. Without it, each infection quietly worsens the scarring on your valve. The duration is usually 10 years from the last episode of rheumatic fever, or until age 40, or longer in high-risk patients. Never miss an injection without speaking to your clinician.
Streptococcal throat infection — act early:
Any throat infection with fever, painful swallowing, or swollen glands must be treated promptly with antibiotics. Do not wait. In Kenya, access to treatment within 24–48 hours of a sore throat is the single most effective prevention strategy for further RHD damage.

Report immediately to your clinician if you develop: Fever with a sore throat · Joint pain (especially knees, ankles, wrists) · A new rash · A nodule under the skin · Involuntary movements of the hands or face (Sydenham's chorea)
Why do I need cardiac rehabilitation?
RHD often results in years of reduced activity as patients avoid exertion because of breathlessness. This causes significant physical deconditioning on top of the cardiac effects of the disease. Cardiac rehabilitation helps restore functional fitness safely within the limits of your valve disease, improves quality of life, and addresses the psychological and social impact of living with a chronic heart condition — particularly important for younger patients whose education, work, and family life are affected.
When is the best time to start?
Start when you are stable — no acute rheumatic fever, no recent hospitalisation for heart failure, and your valve disease is not at the severe symptomatic stage. For mild-to-moderate valve disease, rehabilitation can begin at any point once medically stable. Your programme will be carefully calibrated to your specific valve condition — patients with mitral stenosis, for example, must keep their heart rate controlled during exercise.
Key precautions before starting rehabilitation
  • ⛔Do not start exercise if: You have active rheumatic fever · Severe breathlessness at rest · Recent hospitalisation for heart failure within 2 weeks · Uncontrolled rapid heart rate (AF) with resting HR above 110 bpm
  • ⚠For mitral stenosis patients: Keep your heart rate below 100 bpm during all exercise. Avoid high-intensity activity. If you are in atrial fibrillation, use Borg RPE as your exercise guide — not your pulse. Confirm rate control with your clinician before starting.
  • ✓Anticoagulation check: If you are on warfarin (for AF or mechanical valve), confirm your INR is within target range before starting your exercise programme. INR should be checked within 1–2 weeks of beginning rehabilitation.
Endocarditis prevention — critical for all RHD patients
Infective endocarditis is a serious and potentially fatal infection of the already-damaged heart valves. RHD patients are at significantly higher risk.

Your most important protection:
Brush teeth twice daily and floss regularly · Visit a dentist every 6 months · Always tell your dentist and any doctor or surgeon that you have rheumatic heart disease · You may need antibiotics before dental procedures — discuss with your cardiologist · Report any unexplained fever lasting more than 2–3 days · Report any new or changing heart murmur noticed by a doctor
Living with RHD — social and practical impact
RHD in Kenya disproportionately affects young people — students, parents, and workers. The physical and psychological burden is significant. Moyo Smart cardiac rehabilitation addresses not only your physical recovery but also your ability to return to work, education, and family life. Speak openly with your rehabilitation team about the practical challenges you face — managing the condition in a low-resource setting is part of what we are here to support you with.
